Gear Types Guide

Spur Gears

The most common type of gears with teeth parallel to the axis of rotation. They are efficient for parallel shaft applications with high power transmission.

Helical Gears

Similar to spur gears but with angled teeth. They provide smoother, quieter operation and better load distribution. Used in automotive transmissions.

Bevel Gears

Conical gears used to transmit motion between intersecting shafts at any angle. Common in differential assemblies and angle drives.

Internal Gears

Ring gears with teeth pointing inward. Used in planetary gear systems and compact gear arrangements. Mesh with external pinions.

Planetary Gears

A system consisting of sun, planet, and ring gears. Provides multiple speed ratios and compact design. Used in automatics and multi-speed transmissions.

Rack Gears

Linear gear with teeth cut along its length. Meshes with a pinion to convert rotary to linear motion or vice versa. Used in steering systems and lifts.

Worm Gears

Screw-like gear that meshes with a worm wheel. Provides high reduction ratios with compact design. Self-locking characteristic is beneficial in safety applications.

Parameter Definitions

Number of Teeth

The count of teeth on the gear. More teeth create a larger gear diameter for the same module.

Module

The ratio of pitch diameter to number of teeth. Determines tooth size. Standard metric values: 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12, 16, 20.

Pressure Angle

The angle between the tooth surface and the pitch circle. Standard values are 20¬∞ (most common), 14.5¬∞ (older), and 25¬∞ (heavy-duty).

Face Width

The length of the gear teeth along the axis of rotation. Affects load capacity and noise characteristics.

Bore Diameter

The diameter of the center hole for mounting on a shaft. Must be less than the gear's root diameter.

Helix Angle

For helical gears, the angle at which teeth are cut relative to the axis. Typical range: 15-30¬∞.

Pitch Angle

For bevel gears, the angle between intersecting shaft axes. Directly affects the gear mesh geometry.

Standards & References

ISO 53:1998

Defines tooth forms of cylindrical gears with involute flanks. Specifies gear tooth geometry for spur, helical, and other gear types.

ISO 21771:2007

Covers cylindrical gears with involute flanks for general applications. Includes quality and manufacturing specifications.

Involute Profile

The most common tooth profile, where teeth follow an involute of a circle. Provides consistent velocity ratios and smooth meshing across various center distances.

Module System

The metric gear system using module (mm) instead of diametral pitch. Simplifies calculations and is the international standard.

Export Formats

STEP Files (.step)

Standard for the Exchange of Product Data. Universal CAD format compatible with virtually all CAD software. Best for design review and modification.

STL Files (.stl)

Stereolithography format for 3D printing and rapid prototyping. Available in both ASCII and binary formats. Ideal for 3D printing and simulation.

Usage Tips

Use STEP for CAD modifications and manufacturing. Use STL for 3D printing and FEA analysis. Always verify dimensions and tooth profiles in your CAD software before manufacturing.

Design Guidelines

Module Selection

Choose module based on power transmission needs and space constraints. Larger modules support higher loads.

Tooth Count Ratios

Ensure minimum teeth counts (8+ for spur) to avoid interference. High reduction ratios may require multiple stages.

Material Considerations

Steel gears handle higher loads and speeds. Aluminum suits light-duty applications. Verify material fatigue limits for your application.

Bore Size

Leave adequate rim thickness between bore and root diameter. Typically 1.5-2√ó module as minimum rim thickness.

Manufacturing Notes

Cutting Methods

Gears can be produced by hobbing, shaping, grinding, or 3D printing. Tolerance and surface finish depend on manufacturing method.

Quality Control

Verify tooth profile, surface finish, and dimensional accuracy per ISO standards before assembly.

Assembly Tips

Ensure proper bearing support, alignment, and lubrication for optimal gear performance and longevity.

Typical Tolerances

AGMA Class 8: Commercial gears. Class 10: Good precision. Class 12: High precision (typically ¬±0.02-0.03mm for module 2).

Frequently Asked Questions

What formats can I export? +

Gear Engine supports STEP (.step) and STL (.stl) formats. STEP is ideal for CAD integration and manufacturing, while STL is perfect for 3D printing and simulation.

What gear types are supported? +

We support 7 gear types: Spur (most common), Helical (higher precision), Bevel (angled shafts), Internal (enclosed), Planetary (compact), Rack (linear motion), and Worm (high reduction).

Can I save my designs? +

Yes! Use the "Save Preset" button to save your current parameters. Your presets are stored locally in your browser and can be loaded anytime. You can also save multiple presets with different names.

What standards are supported? +

Gear Engine complies with ISO 53:1998 (involute tooth profiles) and ISO 21771:2007 (bevel gear specifications) for professional-grade gear generation.

How do I share a design? +

After generating a gear, click "Share Design" to get a URL with your parameters encoded. You can send this link to others and they'll see the exact same gear in their browser.

Is this open source? +

Yes! Gear Engine is 100% open source under the MIT license. You can view the source code, contribute, and use it in your own projects.

Gear Terminology Glossary

Module (m)

The ratio of pitch diameter to number of teeth. Smaller modules create finer, smaller gears.

Pitch Diameter

The diameter of the imaginary circle around which teeth engage. It determines gear speed ratios.

Pressure Angle

The angle between the tooth profile and a radial line. Standard is 20¬∞. Affects load distribution and efficiency.

Backlash

The small gap between mating teeth. Prevents binding and compensates for thermal expansion.

Face Width

The length of the tooth along the axis of rotation. Larger face width increases load capacity.

Involute Profile

The mathematical curve shape of gear teeth. Provides smooth, efficient power transmission.

Helix Angle

The angle of teeth on helical gears. Creates smoother engagement than spur gears.

Tip Radius

The radius at the outermost point of the tooth. Affects tooth strength and interference.
